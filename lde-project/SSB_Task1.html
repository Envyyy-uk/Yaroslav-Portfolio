Task 1: e-Commerce Website builders

The modern market offers a multitude of solutions for creating online stores. Most of them fall into the category of SaaS (Software as a Service) — platforms that provide hosting, software, and technical support for a monthly fee. Let's examine two key players in this segment: Shopify and BigCommerce.

1. Shopify: The All-in-One Market Leader

Shopify is the most popular SaaS platform, dominating due to its exceptional simplicity and focus on small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) as well as brands selling directly to consumers (D2C).

Key Benefits Key Drawbacks
Exceptional ease of use: Intuitive, logical admin panel that requires no technical knowledge to run a store. Transaction fees: Charge an additional fee (0.5%-2%) on sales if you don't use their Shopify Payments payment system.
Huge App Store: Thousands of apps to extend functionality (SEO, loyalty programs, dropshipping) that are missing in the basic version. Cost of apps: Many important features (e.g., extended product variants) are only available through paid apps, which increases the monthly cost.
Design and themes: A large selection of modern, professional, and mobile themes. Limited basic functionality: Compared to competitors, the basic version may seem limited (e.g., limit on the number of product variants).
Integrated Payments: Shopify Payments' own payment system makes it easy to accept payments without third-party gateways. "Locked" SEO structure: Has a rigid URL structure (e.g., the /products/ prefix) that cannot be changed, which can be a drawback for advanced SEO.
Scalability: Ideal for beginners but also offers the powerful Shopify Plus enterprise plan for large, high-volume brands.

2. BigCommerce: Power and B2B flexibility

BigCommerce positions itself as a more flexible solution aimed at fast-growing businesses and the B2B (business-to-business) segment. Its main advantage is more built-in features without the need for apps.

Pros Cons
Zero transaction fees: BigCommerce doesn’t charge any own transaction fees, regardless of which payment gateway you use. Sales volume limits: Plans are limited by annual sales volume. If you exceed the limit, you’ll automatically be upgraded to a more expensive plan.
Powerful built-in functionality: Many of the things Shopify requires apps to do (like extended product variants, complex discounts, customer groups for B2B) are built in out of the box. Learning curve: The platform is considered more difficult for beginners than Shopify due to its greater number of features.
SEO flexibility: Gives you full control over your URL structure, which is a plus for search engine optimization. Smaller ecosystem: The selection of themes and apps is smaller than Shopify's.

Conclusion

Shopify is a great choice for beginners and D2C brands that value simplicity and speed of launch above all else. BigCommerce is a better choice for established businesses, companies with complex catalogues, and B2B that want to avoid transaction fees.
